# Campus AI Curriculum Task Force: Member Profiles & Analysis

**Research Date**: November 23, 2025
**Researcher**: Agent Beta
**Purpose**: Stakeholder analysis for AI curriculum proposal development

---

## Executive Summary

### Key Expertise Clusters

The 15-member Campus AI Curriculum Task Force brings together expertise across five critical domains:

1. **AI Technical Expertise** (6 members): Computer vision, multimodal learning, learning analytics, digital agriculture, spatial AI
2. **Curriculum Development & Pedagogy** (5 members): Educational strategy, instructional design, learning environments, equity-focused teaching
3. **Business & Professional Education** (2 members): Online program development, stackable credentials, industry partnerships
4. **Strategic Leadership** (4 members): Graduate education, library services, strategic initiatives, philosophical frameworks
5. **Cross-cutting Themes**: Equity and access (4 members), interdisciplinary collaboration (8 members), industry engagement (5 members)

### Critical Insights

**Gies Business School Representation**: Amanda Brantner and Nerissa Brown bring substantial experience in scalable online education, stackable credential models, and cross-college partnerships. Gies has been a leader in AI/analytics curriculum development, with established programs in Business Analytics and AI-integrated coursework.

**Equity & Access Focus**: Multiple members (Lewis, Magee, Brown, Batzloff) have demonstrated commitments to educational equity, suggesting the committee will prioritize inclusive AI curriculum design.

**Technical-Pedagogical Balance**: The task force balances deep technical AI expertise with educational research, ensuring both technical rigor and pedagogical soundness.

**Industry-Academic Bridge**: Several members (Reid, Hoiem, Ersoz) have significant industry experience, facilitating practical, workforce-relevant curriculum design.

## Potential Perspectives & Priorities

### Equity & Access (Strong Constituency)

**Champions**: Lewis, Magee, Batzloff, Brown
**Perspective**: AI education must be accessible and inclusive, not replicating existing inequities in CS education.

**Key Concerns**:
- Who has access to AI curriculum? (prerequisites, scheduling, cost)
- Do teaching methods create barriers for underrepresented groups?
- How does curriculum shape student identity and belonging?
- What support structures are needed for diverse learners?

**Likely Proposals**:
- Inclusive pedagogy training for AI instructors
- Multiple pathways into AI education (not just CS prerequisites)
- Attention to curriculum language and cultural assumptions
- Support programs for underrepresented students

**Potential Friction**:
- Tension with "rigor" arguments
- Resource requirements for support programs
- Pace of implementation (thoroughness vs. speed)

---

### Technical Rigor (Strong Constituency)

**Champions**: Hoiem, Lourentzou, Wang
**Perspective**: AI education must be technically sound and prepare students for advanced work.

**Key Concerns**:
- Mathematical and computational foundations
- Depth vs. breadth in curriculum
- Keeping pace with rapidly evolving field
- Maintaining Illinois' reputation for excellence

**Likely Proposals**:
- Strong foundational requirements (math, programming, statistics)
- Hands-on projects with real AI systems
- Advanced electives for specialization
- Research opportunities integrated with curriculum

**Potential Friction**:
- Prerequisites may limit access
- Depth requirements may slow implementation
- Specialization vs. general education goals

---

### Applied & Workforce-Focused (Strong Constituency)

**Champions**: Reid, Ersoz, Brantner, Brown
**Perspective**: AI curriculum must prepare students for careers and real-world applications.

**Key Concerns**:
- Alignment with labor market needs
- Practical skills and tools students can use immediately
- Industry partnerships for internships and projects
- Credential recognition by employers

**Likely Proposals**:
- Stackable credentials and micro-credentials
- Industry advisory boards for curriculum
- Capstone projects with real clients
- Professional development and soft skills
- Domain-specific AI applications (agriculture, business, etc.)

**Potential Friction**:
- Tension between workforce training and liberal education
- Risk of curriculum becoming too narrow/vocational
- Industry influence on academic program

---

### Pedagogical Innovation (Moderate Constituency)

**Champions**: Paquette, Magee, Lewis
**Perspective**: How we teach AI matters as much as what we teach.

**Key Concerns**:
- Evidence-based teaching methods
- Use of AI tools in teaching AI
- Learning analytics and personalization
- Assessment that measures real understanding
- Collaborative and active learning

**Likely Proposals**:
- AI tutoring systems and personalized feedback
- Project-based and problem-based learning
- Peer instruction and collaborative problem-solving
- Formative assessment throughout curriculum
- Research on effectiveness of AI education approaches

**Potential Friction**:
- Innovative methods may require instructor training
- Assessment changes may face resistance
- Technology requirements and costs

---

### Ethical & Social (Moderate Constituency)

**Champions**: Scharp, Magee, Batzloff
**Perspective**: AI education must address ethical implications and societal impacts.

**Key Concerns**:
- AI bias, fairness, and discrimination
- Privacy and surveillance
- Impact on labor and society
- Long-term risks (superintelligence, autonomous weapons)
- Environmental impacts of AI

**Likely Proposals**:
- Ethics integrated throughout curriculum (not just one course)
- Critical analysis of AI's social impacts
- Case studies of AI failures and harms
- Philosophical foundations of AI
- Responsible AI development practices

**Potential Friction**:
- Time constraints in already-packed technical curriculum
- Perception as "soft" content vs. technical skills
- Defining boundaries of ethics content

---

### Interdisciplinary & Cross-College (Strong Constituency)

**Champions**: Wang, Ersoz, Brantner, Reid
**Perspective**: AI is relevant across all disciplines, not just CS.

**Key Concerns**:
- AI literacy for non-CS majors
- Domain-specific AI applications
- Collaboration across colleges
- Avoiding AI education silos

**Likely Proposals**:
- Core AI literacy for all students
- Domain-specific AI tracks (AI for agriculture, AI for business, AI for social science, etc.)
- Team-taught courses bringing together disciplinary and AI expertise
- Cross-college certificates and programs
- Stackable credentials applicable to multiple degrees

**Potential Friction**:
- Coordination challenges across units
- Resource allocation and credit sharing
- Different colleges have different standards
- Risk of diluting technical rigor in broad literacy courses

---

### Scalability & Online Delivery (Strong Constituency)

**Champions**: Brantner, Brown, Wang
**Perspective**: AI curriculum must reach large numbers of students efficiently.

**Key Concerns**:
- Serving working professionals and non-traditional students
- Geographic reach beyond Champaign-Urbana
- Sustainability of in-person only models
- Revenue generation for program sustainability

**Likely Proposals**:
- Online and hybrid degree programs
- Asynchronous and self-paced options
- Stackable certificates leading to degrees
- Partnerships with industry for student recruitment
- Technology-enhanced learning (AI tutors, autograders)

**Potential Friction**:
- Quality concerns with online education
- Faculty workload and compensation for online teaching
- Assessment integrity in remote settings
- On-campus students feeling de-prioritized

- Resolution: Frame as "professional competence" not job training

- Administrators (Fulmer) may want quick implementation
- Equity/pedagogy researchers want careful, evidence-based design
- Resolution: Phased rollout with continuous improvement

**Resource Competition**:
- Each college wants AI programs in their unit
- Centralized vs. distributed models
- Resolution: Core curriculum + college-specific specializations
